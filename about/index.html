A friend recently convinced me that I should answer a series of questions and post my answers publically on the 'about' page of my website. She said that it would provide a more accurate and genuine self-description than the bio I've included above. I decided to take her advice.

I've adapted the questions from this wonderful New York Times piece. Because the questions are largely oriented towards couples gauging their compatibility for one another, I've only answered a small subset of the questions.

Before making a telephone call, do you ever rehearse what you are going to say? Why?

Sometimes. I don't go so far as to write a script, but I do like to plan in my head what I'd like to say. I do this mostly because I don't want to waste the time of the person I'm calling.

When I'm calling someone for the first time, I'll usually plan my 'opening statement' before calling as well as a few talking points (just to make sure the conversation keeps moving). That being said, when I call family members, close collaborators, or friends, I don't take the time to plan out everything I'm going to say. When I call these people, I already know what I'd like to say, and there is room for error.


When did you last sing to yourself? To someone else?

I do not have the voice of an angel. To spare the ears and sanity of those around me, I try not to sing aloud. I might make an exception for a shower every once in awhile (the sound of the shower very effectively masks the imperfections in my voice).


If you were able to live to the age of 90 and retain either the mind or body of a 30-year-old for the last 60 years of your life, which would you want?

While this question invokes thought about the value of one's physical ability (and all of its elements, particularly mobility and physical ability) versus the value of one's mental ability, I think the premise is somewhat flawed: I don't believe that the difference in mental ability between the ages of 30 and 90 is equal to the difference in physical ability between 30 and 90.

While I recognize that this is a purely qualitative comparison, I consider the mental ability of a 90 year old to be not significantly less than the mental ability of a 30 year old, provided that the person has not been affected by any sort of abnormal condition in their old age.

The degradation of one's physical ability, however, seems to be much greater: most 90 year olds have trouble performing the most simple day to day tasks (for example, getting out of bed or climbing stairs).

If the objective is to maximize one's fulfillment (which, to me, is strongly correlated with my productivity, which is itself strongly correlated—perhaps in equal parts—to my physical and mental ability), the clear choice is to retain the body of the 30 year old and simply hope that one's cognitive ability is able to widthstand the years.


If you could change anything about the way you were raised, what would it be?

I'm extremely lucky to have had a normal and healthy childhood. I suppose the only thing I would change is the languages I was exposed to as a child. While I was exposed to tens—if not hundreds—of languages as a child (by virtue of growing up in New York City), I was raised monolingually. I would love to be able to speak other languages natively.
